Diptych Drawing

work: drawing (sketch, ink wash)

This drawing took about 30 hrs. I had three goals for it: to use a medium/technique I hadn't used before, to create visual tension, and to evoke interesting thoughts for at least 60 seconds of looking. The high level idea here was to contrast geometric with natural—consistent, precise, right angled vs inconsistent, curved—while creating cohesion with the shared use of black inkwash and the same red. (I very much had this quote in mind throughout.) Additional conceptual elements: both images have complexity abstracted away; the geometric image has no motion while the natural image does. Also, I chose to glue on red paper vs painting it on directly to evoke a sense of transience in the buds of the tree. More literally, this is a contrast between Huntsman Hall at the Wharton School and an Eastern Redbud on the campus outside. It's a diptych (art jargon meaning two images). Inspiration (people I copied/thought of): Bada Shanren, Prof. Mark McGinnis, James Tissot, Edward Hopper.

Lilypad LED Glove

work: concept, wiring, programming

Computers on clothing. How cool is that!? I'm a teaching assistant for a design class at Penn (taught by Orkan Telhan and Yasmin Kafai). I led a session (slides w/ images) introducing the Lilypad: a micro-controller like an Arduino but that can be sewed onto clothes with silver-laden thread to create circuits. This was a simple example I used to kick off the session, and to spark thoughts about things the students could make.

Trim Truck

work: concept, need finding,
ideation process, presentation

Have you had a bad haircut before? It totally sucks (fun video). The idea for the Trim Truck (slides) is that you sign up for a membership which entitles you to as many trims as you'd like. The app shows you where the truck is on any given day. If it's near by & you have 15 min, you drop in. With a weekly trim, your hair stays the perfect length & you never need a risky haircut.

Time Left clock

work: concept, design,
electronics, software, assembly

You've heard the adage "time is money" right? Me too. The goal of this clock is to make us think of time as a resource, and to keep track of how much we have left to spend. To do this, it presents five familiar circular clock faces displaying unfamiliar units of time—a month, a week, a year, etc. The "100%" at the top indicates to a new viewer how those units of time are being measured. I worked with 2 other students.

Pixels cereal

work: concept, visual design,
packaging, baking cereal

Wouldn't it be cool if you could eat color? More specifically, what if you could eat a digital construct: a pixel. That's the idea here. Instead of eating strawberry or chocolate cereal, you could have green or RGB or CMYK. The package is inteded to communicate the idea to a viewer, and inside was actual cereal I prototyped. The concept was provoked when I heard about the New Aesthetic art movement.
